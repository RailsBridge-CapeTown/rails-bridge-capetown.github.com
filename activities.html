Activity List

For each activity, have instructions printed out and up on a wall.

These are ideas and options: don't try and do them all! :)

Warm-up exercises (before the workshop)

What you know, and what’s your plan

Send students an email, asking

  • “What are three things you already know about programming?”
  • “What do you plan to do with what you learn?”

During the workshop

Share your answers

Each student has to ask three others about their answers to the warm-up questions, and write them down on sticky notes.

What does a developer look like?

Students grab sticky notes and pens and write down answers to “What does a developer look like?”.
On a separate table have cards with questions: each question requires a google search to find the answer (which is a developer somewhere in the world).
Students compare their answers to the person they had found on the internet.

What does an engineer look like?"

Students grab sticky notes and pens and write down answers to “What does an engineer look like?”

Students look up #ILookLikeAnEngineer, find an awesome person there, and introduce them to the person sitting next to them.

Good, bad, change

Students write down:

  • one good thing;
  • one bad thing;
  • one thing they would change.

They write one point, switch cards with someone, and write the next one.

Pass That Card

  • Write down a question you have.
  • Take a card with a question on, and answer it.
  • Take a card with an answer on, and write “agree” or “disagree.”

What Three Words

  • Write down three things you've learned this morning, one per sticky note.
  • Discuss them with a partner.

Warm-up survey

  • Find 3 people.
  • Write down 1 warm-up answer from each on a sticky note.

What Makes a good Web App?

  • On sticky notes, write at least three things that a good web app needs.

Take A Stand

  • Students stand on a line from not so confident to very confident
  • Students pair up from opposite end of the line to discuss their problems and ideas for solutions

After the workshop

Hey, buddy

  • Students Write down a SMART goal on the front of a card.
  • Students write down their name and email address on the back of a card.
  • Students swap cards with a buddy, and agree to email each other during the next week.
A SMART goal is Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound.

Valuable

  • On your own, write on a card the most valuable thing you've learned today.
  • In groups of 3 or more, discuss your cards.
